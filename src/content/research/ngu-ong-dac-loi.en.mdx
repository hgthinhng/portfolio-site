<ThesisBox>
While the US, Israel, and Iran absorb the costs of conflict, Russia quietly accumulates the windfall. Through the oil channel, sustained Middle East instability translates directly into higher Brent prices, a reversal of the Urals discount, and a multi-billion dollar fiscal tailwind — all without firing a shot in the theater.
</ThesisBox>

## The Mechanism

The conflict creates a persistent risk premium on Brent crude. Higher Brent lifts the floor price for all crude benchmarks. Crucially, the Urals–Brent spread — which blew out to a ~$29 (42%) discount after the 2022 sanctions — has compressed dramatically in the Asian market as Chinese and Indian refiners compete for the discounted barrel.

<PullQuote>
"Urals, once trading at a 42% discount to Brent in Europe, has flipped to a $4–5 **premium** in Asian spot markets — a $33 swing that compounds directly into Russian fiscal revenue."
</PullQuote>

At any sustained Brent above $59/bbl, Russia runs a fiscal surplus on its oil revenue line. At $119/bbl, the surplus is roughly $60/bbl — a daily windfall in the tens of billions of dollars across Russia's ~10 mb/d production base, even after accounting for production costs and logistic discounts.

## Risk Factors

<RiskNote>
**Key risks to the thesis:** (1) A ceasefire or US-brokered de-escalation collapses the war risk premium and returns Brent toward pre-conflict levels; (2) OPEC+ spare capacity deployment — Saudi Arabia holds ~3 mb/d — can cap price appreciation; (3) Demand destruction from a prolonged conflict tips the global economy into stagflation, ultimately compressing oil demand faster than supply shrinks.
</RiskNote>

## Conclusion

Russia's posture — selling oil at elevated prices to sanctions-agnostic Asian buyers while Western sanctions have structurally reduced the Urals supply to European markets — positions it as the structural beneficiary of sustained Middle East instability. The fiscal math is straightforward: every dollar above the $59/bbl breakeven accrues directly to the state budget.

The thesis draws on 20+ primary sources across energy markets, geopolitics, and Russian fiscal data — and is designed to be read as a standalone, written investment view, not a trading signal.
